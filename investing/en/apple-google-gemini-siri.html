Apple may abandon its models to use Google Gemini for Siri

Bloomberg reported that Apple is in discussions with Google about using Google’s Gemini artificial intelligence to power a revamped Siri. The talks would involve building a custom AI model for Siri and reflect Apple’s effort to upgrade its voice assistant amid rapid advances in generative AI. The report frames the negotiations as a potential strategic shift for Apple, which has historically emphasized proprietary systems and on-device processing.

According to Bloomberg, Google has already begun training a version of Gemini that could run on Apple’s servers. Apple has not yet decided whether to adopt an external partner’s model or continue developing its own internal models for Siri. Prior exploratory talks reportedly included other AI providers, such as OpenAI and Anthropic, but those efforts encountered financial and strategic headwinds. Both Apple and Google declined to comment on the Bloomberg report.

The sequence in the reporting is straightforward: Apple sought outside partnerships as it judged its existing Siri capabilities insufficient for current AI benchmarks; it held talks with multiple providers; Bloomberg says Google moved to adapt Gemini to Apple’s environment; and Apple remains undecided about a final path. The report does not identify contract terms, technical specifications, or a timeline for a decision. The only confirmed public detail is that talks are ongoing and that both companies have declined public comment.

Technically, training a version of Gemini to run on Apple servers implies work on model portability and deployment tailored to Apple’s hardware and software stack. Bloomberg’s account suggests Google is preparing a variant that could integrate with Apple’s infrastructure, rather than offering a generic, cloud-only service. The report does not specify whether that variant would execute entirely on-device, partially on Apple servers, or via a mixed approach, nor does it describe data-handling specifics.

Structurally, the reported discussions highlight a tension in Apple’s AI strategy: preserve control and user privacy through proprietary models and on-device processing, or accelerate capability gains by incorporating externally developed large language models. Experts cited in the reporting frame any partnership with Google as a notable concession by Apple to rely on a major competitor’s technology, with attendant trade-offs between speed-to-market and architectural independence.

User-facing consequences described in the report are potential improvements to Siri’s contextual understanding and generative capabilities, producing richer interactions. At the same time, observers flag three categories of risk: privacy implications depending on how data is processed and stored; engineering challenges in integrating a complex external model into Apple’s tightly controlled ecosystem; and strategic dependency that could limit Apple’s future options or bargaining power.

Looking ahead, the key variables for Apple are the balance of privacy protections, technical feasibility, and strategic cost versus benefit. Apple could decide to continue developing internal models, to enter a partnership that customizes an external model for its environment, or to pursue a hybrid approach. The Bloomberg report provides no indication of which outcome is likeliest.

Market and regulatory actors will watch Apple’s decision for signals about the direction of voice assistants and device-level AI. A partnership with Google would reshape expectations about vendor boundaries in AI stacks, while a continued internal push would reaffirm Apple’s longstanding emphasis on proprietary control. Either path will influence competitors’ strategies and how consumers experience generative AI features on personal devices.
