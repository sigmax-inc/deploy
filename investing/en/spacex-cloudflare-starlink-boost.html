SpaceX and Cloudflare join to boost Starlink internet speeds

SpaceX has begun a partnership with Cloudflare to accelerate its Starlink satellite internet service, according to a report by The Information. The collaboration centers on deploying additional mini data centers globally to improve Starlink’s network performance. Neither company has disclosed technical specifics or the commercial terms of the agreement, and both declined to provide detailed public comment on the scope of the work.

The Information’s report frames the deal as a move to augment Starlink’s existing infrastructure by placing Cloudflare-managed edge facilities closer to end users. Industry commentary in the report highlights that a greater number of strategically located mini data centers can lower round-trip times for traffic, which translates into reduced latency and higher effective throughput for customers. The report did not specify the number, locations or timelines for deploying these facilities.

Market reaction was immediate. Shares of Cloudflare rose by more than 7% after the report was published. The price movement reflects investor expectations that a partnership with a major consumer-facing network like Starlink could expand Cloudflare’s addressable market for edge services and content delivery. The Information did not provide details about any revenue-sharing or capacity-commitment arrangements that might underpin such investor expectations.

Starlink, operated by SpaceX, is already a leading provider in the consumer satellite broadband market, competing with initiatives such as Amazon’s Project Kuiper, the UK’s OneWeb and commercial satellite operator Planet. The addition of mini data centers through Cloudflare would aim to sharpen Starlink’s performance differential in latency-sensitive applications, potentially strengthening its position against those competitors, depending on deployment scale and integration speed.

Cloudflare has established its business model around content delivery, DDoS protection and edge compute, using distributed facilities to cache content and terminate sessions closer to users. The Information’s description implies Cloudflare would apply that model to Starlink traffic, effectively creating more local points where satellite traffic can be handed off to terrestrial networks or processed at the edge. Whether the work will focus on caching, route optimization, edge computing or a combination of functions was not reported.

Recent usage trends cited by Cloudflare in public data show Starlink’s global internet traffic more than tripled in 2024. That rise in traffic aligns with growing demand for satellite internet in regions lacking reliable terrestrial infrastructure and for mobility use cases. The volume growth provides a contextual rationale for investing in edge capacity: higher traffic increases the value of reducing latency and improving throughput at scale.

Operational and commercial integration between a satellite operator and a CDN provider involves technical and regulatory coordination, including peering arrangements, traffic engineering and potential locality constraints. The Information’s report did not address those operational complexities or indicate how licensing, spectrum or local regulations might affect deployment choices in different countries.

For customers, the immediate practical outcome would be faster and more consistent performance if Cloudflare’s edge facilities reduce latency and increase effective bandwidth. For the companies, successful implementation could mean lower transit costs for SpaceX and expanded service offerings for Cloudflare. For competitors, the move presents an additional factor to consider when positioning their own networks and partnerships.

Looking ahead, the partnership’s impact will depend on deployment scale, geographic coverage and technical integration details that remain undisclosed. Observers will watch whether the companies announce precise timelines, locations, commercial terms or technical performance targets. Without those details, the reported collaboration signals intent to prioritize edge capacity for satellite broadband but leaves the magnitude and timing of improvements uncertain.
