OpenAI cofounder raises one billion to build safety first AI lab

Ilya Sutskever, former chief scientist and co‑founder of OpenAI, has launched Safe Superintelligence Inc. (SSI) and raised $1 billion in its first major funding round. The company was valued at about $5 billion after three months of operation. SSI was co‑founded with Daniel Levy and Daniel Gross and attracted investment from Andreessen Horowitz, Sequoia Capital and other venture firms. The startup positions itself explicitly as safety‑focused.

SSI was established in June 2024 after Sutskever departed OpenAI amid internal conflicts over the future direction of AI development and safety. The initial team comprised roughly 10 employees. The company opened offices in California and Tel Aviv and has signaled plans to expand headcount by recruiting researchers and engineers. The recent financing is described by SSI as its first major funding round.

SSI’s stated mission is to develop safe and aligned superintelligent AI systems—models that not only outperform humans across tasks but are engineered to act in alignment with human values and safety requirements. The company contrasts this objective with the prevailing industry emphasis on consumer and enterprise applications, framing its work as foundational research on alignment rather than product acceleration.

Sutskever has said SSI is “not trying to go down the same path faster” and that the founders seek a “straight shot to safe superintelligence.” Those phrases signal an intent to change development priorities and methods rather than to replicate existing approaches at greater speed. SSI describes its strategy as a reinvention of how safety‑focused AI is built.

Analysts interpret SSI’s funding and explicit safety focus as evidence of two market dynamics: intensifying competition for top AI talent and growing concern about the risks posed by more capable AI systems. The $1 billion backing is read as investor confidence in Sutskever’s vision for foundational advances in AI safety as technology progresses and public apprehension increases.

The circumstances of Sutskever’s departure from OpenAI and SSI’s launch suggest a strategic divergence within the AI field between product‑oriented firms and safety‑oriented efforts. The substantial early funding indicates investors see value in a distinct, safety‑first organization, either as a practical route to safer systems or as a way to influence industry norms and priorities.

Operationally, SSI’s rapid valuation and expansion plans are likely to intensify competition for researchers and engineers, with effects on hiring at other AI labs. The presence of offices in two technology hubs points to an intent to build a bicontinental research footprint. SSI’s focus on alignment research may also attract academic collaborators and shape technical agendas within the safety community.

In the near term SSI will concentrate on recruiting talent and advancing its technical approach to alignment, supported by the $1 billion round. Its success will hinge on whether it can translate that talent and capital into new safety methods. The company’s emergence adds a pronounced safety‑driven actor to an AI landscape increasingly divided between commercial scale‑up and foundational risk mitigation, a dynamic that investors, competitors and regulators will monitor.
