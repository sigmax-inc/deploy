Why is OpenAI choosing to open its first office in New Delhi now?

OpenAI’s plan to open its first official office in New Delhi next year feels less like putting up another sign and more like moving into the neighborhood. The company is clearly signaling a commitment to be present on the ground: assembling a local team, engaging with government, industry, developers and academia. Bringing in Pragya Misra to lead public policy and partnerships—after roles at Truecaller and Meta—and naming Rishi Jaitly, who ran Twitter India, as an advisor underscores that intent. Promises to support models across Indian languages, host developer events, and roll out India-specific pricing — even lower-cost tiers tailored for the market — all point to a genuine effort to get closer to users.

Why now, and why New Delhi? It’s not just market expansion. It reads as a deliberate attempt to bake Indian voices and needs into products. That requires people who understand local politics, social norms and usage habits. Hiring seasoned local experts and building a diverse on-the-ground team isn’t about headcount; it’s about creating channels for real conversation. You can’t adapt languages, cultural norms, pricing and support from a desk thousands of miles away — you need to listen, iterate and fix things based on actual experience.

This move also follows a broader industry trend: AI companies are racing to align with local languages, regulations and expectations. Competitors like Google and Microsoft have already invested heavily in regional partnerships and language optimization. OpenAI’s approach—partnering with government and recruiting regional talent—looks like an attempt to carve a path that combines technical work with local trust-building. The office will be more than a workspace; it’s a portal for regular, candid dialogue.

What will change if this works? With local veterans steering the effort and closer coordination among government, businesses and universities, the product will start to feel more Indian: tone, phrasing, UI choices and even pricing that better reflect everyday needs. That could make AI feel friendlier and more affordable to many users. At the same time, competition will intensify for Indian startups. Working closely with government can help set standards and build legitimacy early on, but it also raises legitimate concerns about surveillance, misuse and the compression of power around technology. More players on the river makes the channel wider, but it also makes rules and safeguards more important.

Responsibility needs to accompany speed. Without broad social consensus on fair competition, data protection and ethical use, rapid rollout can become a liability. OpenAI’s stated intention to meet developers and local communities and fold their feedback into products and pricing reflects an understanding that respect for language and culture is essential for real adoption. Entering someone’s daily life requires humility: observe more, speak less, and adjust.

Opening a doorway in New Delhi is both a strategic move to root itself in a vast and complex market and an emotional choice to build trust, not just grow users. If it sparks India-specific language models, locally priced plans, and active developer programs, the product could weave into daily routines more quickly and more deeply. At this threshold—where excitement and anxiety coexist—the right approach is steady, curious engagement and constant verification of how technology actually affects people’s lives. This looks like a reasonable first step toward that.
