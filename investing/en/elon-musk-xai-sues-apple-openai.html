Elon Musks xAI sues Apple and OpenAI for alleged AI monopoly

Elon Musk’s AI company xAI has filed a lawsuit in a Texas federal court accusing Apple and OpenAI of conspiring to monopolize the emerging market for consumer-facing artificial intelligence. The complaint alleges the two companies arranged for deep integration of OpenAI’s models into Apple operating systems for iPhones, iPads and Macs and that this arrangement has blocked rivals from meaningful access to Apple’s user base and App Store exposure.

According to the complaint, Apple and OpenAI collaborated to integrate OpenAI technology, including ChatGPT, into Apple devices and services, and used that collaboration to favor OpenAI’s offerings in ways that shut out competing products. xAI says the effect is to deny consumers meaningful alternative AI choices and to make it nearly impossible for competing apps, including xAI’s Grok app, to reach tens of millions of Apple users or to achieve fair placement in App Store rankings.

The complaint frames the dispute as “a tale of two monopolists joining forces to ensure their continued dominance,” and argues that the partnership not only limits distribution but also gives OpenAI access to valuable user data that strengthens its market position. xAI is seeking billions of dollars in damages and asks the court to block Apple and OpenAI from continuing the alleged conduct, and to require changes that would open competition within the AI industry and the App Store ranking system.

The filing centers on two related levers of platform control: integration into the operating system and influence over App Store visibility. Integration can route user interactions and default capabilities toward a particular provider without users having to install or select a third-party app. Control of App Store rankings and featuring determines which apps gain initial discovery and sustained usage. xAI’s legal theory ties both levers to anti-competitive exclusion of rivals.

xAI’s complaint also highlights data access as a competitive advantage. It asserts that privileged integration gives OpenAI exposure to user interactions and metrics that can be used to improve its models and services, reinforcing its lead and creating feedback loops that make late entrants less viable. That argument underscores a structural concern in platform markets where distribution and data privileges can compound into durable dominance.

Apple and OpenAI have not provided a detailed response to the lawsuit as of the filing, and the companies’ public statements are not included in xAI’s complaint. The absence of immediate, detailed responses leaves open procedural and factual disputes that are likely to surface early in litigation, such as the scope of any integration agreements, the mechanics of App Store ranking, and the factual basis for claimed data access and harm.

If the court accepts xAI’s claims, remedies could include monetary damages and injunctive relief aimed at changing how integrations and app rankings operate on Apple platforms. Conversely, if the court rejects the allegations, current arrangements between device makers and AI providers would be likely to persist, maintaining the status quo in distribution and platform control.

The case spotlights broader tensions in the AI market between fast-growing model providers and the platforms that control device access and app distribution. The dispute will be watched for legal precedent on how antitrust law applies to software integrations and data flows, and for its practical implications for where and how consumers encounter AI services on major devices.
