Cloudflare lets websites charge AI bots for crawling access

Cloudflare has introduced Pay Per Crawl, a tool in private beta that lets website owners charge AI bots for access to their content. The feature responds to publisher concerns about AI companies scraping material for model training and other uses without compensation, offering a new mechanism to control and potentially monetize automated crawling.

Under Pay Per Crawl, Cloudflare applies a default block on AI crawlers for protected sites unless site owners grant explicit permission. Site operators can configure access rules with granular options: allow, block, or require payment for each bot visit. The controls are applied at the Cloudflare layer, placing enforcement before content delivery to the requesting crawler.

Publishers can set a flat, per-request price for crawlers that request access. If a crawler fails to meet the set price, Cloudflare returns an HTTP 402 Payment Required response rather than serving the requested content. Cloudflare acts as the intermediary for payment processing and for the technical enforcement of those access decisions.

The system also requires AI clients requesting access to disclose their intended use: model training, inference, or search. That disclosure is designed to give site owners context for their decisions about allowing, blocking, or charging specific crawlers, and to increase transparency around automated access to web content.

Cloudflare positions Pay Per Crawl as a tool to balance negotiating power between large media organizations, which commonly secure direct licensing arrangements, and smaller publishers that have lacked the infrastructure to demand compensation from AI vendors. By providing a standardized technical and payment layer, the tool aims to lower the barrier for smaller sites to monetize automated scraping.

The company and proponents frame the model as a potential source of new revenue for publishers at a time when referral traffic patterns are changing. As AI-generated answers and other non-traditional search behaviors account for a larger share of user queries, some publishers face declining traffic from conventional search engines; Pay Per Crawl is presented as one possible countermeasure to monetize machine access directly.

The feature is currently in private beta, and the public rollout timetable and adoption rates are not disclosed. The announcement does not detail technical limits, enforcement edge cases, or how disputes over crawler identification or declared use will be resolved; those operational aspects will likely determine real-world effectiveness as the product scales.

Key metrics to monitor going forward include publisher adoption of the tool, how AI companies respond to per-request pricing and disclosure requirements, and whether the mechanism influences broader licensing negotiations. Pay Per Crawl introduces a technical path for publishers to assert commercial terms for machine access, but its impact will depend on uptake and on how the ecosystem adapts to a fee-for-crawl model.
