Is Elon Musk suing Apple and OpenAI for excluding rival AI apps from iPhones?

Elon Musk’s xAI filed a lawsuit in a Texas federal court accusing Apple and OpenAI of conspiring to suppress competition in artificial intelligence. The complaint alleges that Apple’s exclusive integration of OpenAI technology into iPhones, via a feature called “Apple Intelligence,” and related App Store practices have denied other AI developers access to large user audiences and distorted app visibility. xAI and Musk’s X Corp. seek billions of dollars in damages and a court order to halt the practices.

According to the complaint, Apple’s arrangement with OpenAI provides ChatGPT with preferential access across iPhones through Apple Intelligence, limiting rival models’ ability to reach millions of users. xAI further alleges that App Store rankings have been manipulated to favor OpenAI-linked offerings, making it nearly impossible for competing chatbots such as xAI’s Grok to appear near the top of charts and achieve equivalent discovery.

The lawsuit frames the conduct as two dominant firms cooperating to defend market power during a period of rapid change in the AI sector. xAI characterizes Apple and OpenAI as “two monopolists” working together to exclude competitors. The company claims this conduct harms consumers by reducing choice and impeding innovation, and it demands both monetary relief and injunctive remedies to restore competitive conditions.

Apple and OpenAI have not issued public responses to the filing. The suit adds to a series of tensions between Musk and OpenAI; Musk was a co‑founder of OpenAI and later departed, citing disagreements about the organization’s direction and commercial structure. The complaint uses that backdrop to underscore longstanding disputes but does not introduce new factual allegations about OpenAI’s internal governance beyond the business arrangement with Apple.

Structurally, xAI’s argument rests on two linked features of modern platform competition: preferential integration at the operating‑system level and control over app distribution channels. Exclusive OS-level features can make a particular service the default or most convenient option on widely used devices, while App Store placement affects user discovery and download rates. xAI asserts that the combined effect forecloses meaningful competitive entry for independent AI developers.

If the court finds merit in xAI’s claims, remedies could target contractual terms, platform integrations, or App Store policies. xAI is seeking an injunction to stop what it describes as anticompetitive conduct in addition to unspecified billions in damages. The filing signals that xAI will pursue both immediate relief and monetary compensation for alleged past harms, but the complaint itself does not specify the precise contractual terms between Apple and OpenAI that underpin the exclusive integration.

The case may prompt scrutiny of how device makers integrate third‑party AI into core system features and how platform gatekeepers manage app visibility. Regulators and competitors have increasingly focused on platform neutrality and distribution practices, and a high‑profile federal lawsuit could influence industry behavior even before a final judgment. However, the complaint does not include regulatory filings or official investigations at this stage.

For developers and consumers, the lawsuit highlights conflicts over access to users and the competitive implications of deep platform integration. For Apple and OpenAI, the suit presents legal and reputational risks that could affect their product strategies. The Texas federal court process will determine whether xAI’s allegations establish unlawful collaboration and whether courts should impose structural or behavioral remedies.
