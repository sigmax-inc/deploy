Why did Elon Musk turn to rival Mark Zuckerberg for help in trying to acquire OpenAI?

The court filings are simple on the surface but weigh heavy. They show Elon Musk tried to buy OpenAI through his company xAI for roughly $97.4 billion and, to bridge that gap, even went straight to longtime rival Mark Zuckerberg asking for an investment or loan. Meta said no, and OpenAI’s board formally blocked the bid. Musk has also sued to stop OpenAI’s restructuring.

Why turn to a competitor? It comes down to scale. A deal of that size can’t be pulled off on one person’s will or one company’s balance sheet. There are only a handful of places that can marshal the cash and massive data-center capacity needed—and Meta was one of them. In plain terms, the stakes were so huge he was willing to fetch water from the enemy camp.

There was strategy behind the move, too. Approaching a rival sends a signal—to the OpenAI board and to the market—that he was serious and willing to up the ante. It’s a pressure play: show you can widen the pot and you gain leverage. Even if the deal fails, having taken every possible route bolsters your negotiating position.

Personal motives matter as well. Musk was a co-founder at OpenAI and left in 2018. Since starting xAI he’s repeatedly argued OpenAI has drifted from its original mission. There’s a mix of rescue impulse and a desire for control—he clearly wants to steer AI’s direction. Reaching out to a competitor underscores how isolated and urgent he must have felt.

In the end, reality won out. Pride, mistrust, conflicts of interest and regulatory risk tangled together. Meta isn’t likely to hand a rival a loaded gun, and OpenAI’s board has to weigh mission, independence and fiduciary duties—not just a dollar figure. This wasn’t a door money alone could pry open.

The episode also signals a broader shift in the industry. Expect more headline-grabbing acquisition attempts as companies fight for models, data and talent. At the same time, antitrust scrutiny and demands for transparency will only get tougher. Investors will get more cautious, and employees will find themselves torn between ideals and commercial pressures.

More than a spat between two billionaires, this story shines a light on an era where power, money and anxiety collide. For a moment, competition looked like partnership—and then fell apart. That single outreach revealed the uneasy heartbeat of an industry on the verge of major rearrangement.
